Messenger RNA (mRNA) is a single-stranded copy of DNA, made up of nucleotides, that holds instructions for the ribosomes of the order of the Amino Acids to make a protein.

mRNA nucleotides are grouped into triplets called codons, and the bases that make up mRNA codons are: Adenine, Guanine, Cytosine and Uracil.

2.
This is a lipid nanoparticle with mRNA tucked inside. Lipid nanoparticles are balls of layered lipids that have their contents tucked inside.

Lipids are nonpolar so they are able to pass through the phospholipid bilayer of the cell membrane. mRNA is polar, which means it cannot pass through the cell membrane and needs to be brought into the cell inside the lipid nanoparticle.

3.
This is a human cell. The cell wraps some of the lipid nanoparticle in the cell membrane to bring into the cytoplasm and then "unpacks" it all, leaving only the mRNA to be read.

This is a representation of the mRNA going into a lipid nanoparticle, and then once injected into the body, the nanoparticle with mRNA going into a cell.
