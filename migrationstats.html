The Atlantic American Flyway

The Atlantic Americas Flyway connects the Canadian Arctic Archipelago to South America’s southern tip, Tierra del Fuego. Numerous arctic breeding birds move south along the flyway for the northern winter. North America’s Atlantic coast is a heavily used avenue of travel by passage migrants. Each spring, thousands of waterfowl and waders follow the coast southward to Florida on their way to wintering quarters in Cuba, Puerto Rico, Central America and South America.

The Atlantic American Flyway hosts over 250 bird species and serves as an important resting and feeding site for migrating and wintering waterfowl, including more than 35,000 Canada geese and 15,000 ducks using the Atlantic Flyway during the fall migration. Some species, such as the Red Knot (Calidris canutus), travel the flyway’s full length twice each year - an incredible round trip of 30,000km. On leaving their breeding grounds Red Knot fly south through northeastern Canada, past Hudson Bay, to the Northeast seaboard of the United States. They gather in huge numbers at a stoppover point, the Delaware Bay. Nearly 90% of the entire population of the Red Knot subspecies 'rufa' can be present on the bay in a single day.

Sources: http://datazone.birdlife.org/userfiles/file/sowb/ flyways/3_Atlantic_Americas_Factsheet.pdf and https://www.allaboutbirds.org/guide/Red_Knot/ overview

The Mississippi American Flyway

The Central Americas Flyway extends over 14,000km from the Canadian Arctic to the southern tip of Argentina. From its northern limit, the flyway stretches south through the North American Interior Plains and the Mississippi Valley to the marshes and prairies of the Gulf Coast. At this point some migrants head straight across the Gulf of Mexico towards the Yucatán Peninsula and the Isthmus of Tehuantepec. Others opt for the longer, yet potentially safer, land route through Mexico and Central America. In South America, the flyway broadens into the Amazon Basin and then continues south through the Gran Chaco and Pampas before terminating in Patagonia.

According to Bird Life International, almost 400 bird species travel the Central American Flyway each year. One of these birds is the rare Swainson's Hawk, an elegant gray, white, and brown hawk, which has one of the longest migrations of any American raptor.

Sources: http://datazone.birdlife.org/userfiles/file/ sowb/flyways/2_Central_Americas_Factsheet.pdf and https://www.allaboutbirds.org/guide/ Swainsons_Hawk/overview

The Pacific American Flyway

The Pacific Americas Flyway stretches from the permafrost tundra of the high arctic, south along the Pacific seaboard to Tierra del Fuego, the southernmost tip of the South American mainland. Many of the millions of migratory waterbirds that nest on Alaska’s expansive tundra embark on remarkable southern journeys along this flyway. Bordered by the Rocky Mountains to the east, the flyway continues down through the USA to Mexico and Central America.

At least 1 billion birds migrate along the Pacific American flyway each year. This includes the Arctic Tern, which travels from its Arctic breeding grounds to Antarctica where it enjoys the Antarctic summer, covering around 25,000 miles.

Sources: http://datazone.birdlife.org/userfiles/file/ sowb/flyways/1_Pacific_Americas_Factsheet.pdf and https://www.allaboutbirds.org/guide/ Arctic_Tern/overview

The East Atlantic Flyway

The East Atlantic flyway links a discontinuous band of arctic breeding grounds that stretch from Canada east to central Siberia with wintering grounds in Western Europe and West Africa. Each autumn, huge numbers of arctic migrants, especially waterbirds, converge on western Europe from the far north. For many arctic migrants, especially ducks, geese and swans, Western Europe is the final destination. For others, it is a stepping stone on a journey that will eventually conclude at wintering grounds in Africa. Here arctic passage migrants are joined by departing temperate breeders also heading towards Africa.

The East Atlantic Flyway is a migration route used by about 90 million birds annually, passing from their breeding areas in the Western Hemisphere (Canada, Greenland, and Iceland) or the Eastern Hemisphere (Scandinavia, Arctic Russia, and Northwest Siberia) to wintering areas in western Europe and on to southern Africa. Most species of birds usually come from either the Western Hemisphere or the Eastern Hemisphere, but some species have one subspecies that has evolved to go east and separate one that goes west. One such example is the Wheatear. There is a "Greenland" Wheatear (Oenanthe oenanthe leucorhoa), which flies west, and it's counterpart, a "Scandinavian" Wheatear (O. o. oenanthe), which flies east.

Source: http://datazone.birdlife.org/userfiles/file/ sowb/flyways/4_East_Atlantic_Factsheet.pdf

The Black Sea/Mediterranean Flyway

The Black Sea/Mediterranean Flyway is one of three Palaearctic-African flyways connectingEurope with Africa. Collectively, these constitute the world’s largest bird migration system. The scale of the avian movement is truly incredible with over 2 billion passerines and near-passerines, 2.5 million ducks and two million raptors migrating from their breeding grounds in Europe and central and western Asia to winter in tropical Africa.

Old World warblers, like the Spectacled Warbler, mostly breed in Europe and Asia and migrate to Africa or tropical Asia for the winter. There are over 250 Old World warblers, most of which migrate on this flyway or the East Africa West Asia flyway.

Sources: http://datazone.birdlife.org/userfiles/file/sowb/ flyways/5_Mediterranean_Black_Sea_Factsheet.pdf and https://www.beautyofbirds.com/ warblersoldworld.html

The Central Asian Flyway

The Central Asian Flyway is the shortest flyway in the world. Lying entirely within the Northern Hemisphere, it connects a large swathe of the Palaearctic with the Indian subcontinent. Separating the subcontinent from the Tibetan Plateau to the north are the Himalayas, which rise to over 8km and stretch 200km from north to south. Many of the migratory birds that breed in the mid-Palaearctic choose to avoid this formidable barrier and instead make a longer, south-westerly flight to Africa for the northern winter.

The Central Asia Flyway covers 182 species of birds, and around 280 populations which includes 29 globally threatened species which breed, migrate and winter within the region. To get to their wintering grounds, some birds, such as the Bar-headed Goose (Anser indicus), follow a route directly over the Himalayan range, rather than going around it. The species is the world's highest-altitude migrant, capable of clearing even Mount Everest.

Sources: http://datazone.birdlife.org/userfiles/file/sowb/ flyways/7_Central_Asia_Factsheet.pdf and https://www.dookinternational.com/blog/central-asia-flyway/

The East Africa-West Asia Flyway

Many birds that breed in the mid-Palaearctic choose to embark on a much longer south-westerly migration to Africa rather than spend the northern winter directly to the south. By travelling westwards along the East Asia/East Africa Flyway, migrating birds avoid the formidable obstacles presented by the Tibetan plateau and Himalayas, which effectively bar direct migration to the south. Migration from the Palaearctic breeding quarters to northeast Africa is generally fast with most birds arriving in northern Sudan and Ethiopia during late August and September.

Over 330 species use this flyway, and seven sites along the route have over a million birds. The Siberian Crane, which travels along this flyway, has one of the longest migrations of any crane species. Unfortunately, it is Critically Endangered and as of 2010, the world population was estimated to be at about 3,200 birds total.

Source: http://datazone.birdlife.org/userfiles/file/sowb/ flyways/6_East_Asia_East_Africa_Factsheet.pdf and https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ Asian%E2%80%93East_African_Flyway

The East Asia-Australian Flyway

The East Asia/Australasia Flyway extends from Arctic Russia and North America to the southern limits of Australia and New Zealand. It encompasses large parts of East Asia, all of Southeast Asia and includes eastern India and the Andaman and Nicobar Islands. The scale of avian movement along the flyway is awesome, with over 50 million migratory waterbirds, including 8 million waders, using the route annually. Flyway population estimates for migratory waders include 2.88 million Oriental Pratincole (Glareola maldivarum) and up to one million Marsh Sandpiper (Tringa stagnatilis).

Very few bird species travel all the way from the Northern Hemisphere to Australia. Only ten species do so regularly, including the Yellow Wagtail (Motacilla flava), Fork-tailed Swift (Apus pacificus) and Oriental Cuckoo (Cuculus optatus), and most are rare or occupy only the northern fringes of the region. Even the Barn Swallow (Hirundo rustica), which elsewhere in the world undertakes considerable journeys to the lower latitudes of the southern hemisphere, only reaches the north-western parts of Australia.

Sources: http://datazone.birdlife.org/userfiles/file/sowb/ flyways/8_East_Asia_Australasia_Factsheet.pdf and https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ East_Asian%E2%80%93Australasian_Flyway
